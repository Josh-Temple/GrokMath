# Unit 12: Operations with Rational Numbers

## Concept Map

- **Bridge from Unit 11:** Rational numbers combine magnitude (fraction/decimal value) with direction (positive or negative).
- **Operation meaning stays consistent:**
  - addition/subtraction track net change,
  - multiplication scales by a factor,
  - division asks how many groups or what unit size fits.
- **Sign patterns are not separate tricks:** They come from number-line movement and inverse relationships between operations.
- **Equivalent forms support fluency:** Fractions, decimals, and signed mixed values can represent the same quantity and help choose efficient computation paths.
- **Connection to next topics:** Reliable rational-number operations support linear equations, coordinate geometry, and function analysis.

## Intuitive Explanation

Imagine keeping score in a game where gains are positive and losses are negative. If your team gains $\frac{3}{4}$ points in one round and loses $\frac{1}{2}$ point in the next, your total is still a single rational number that tells the net result.

Now think about temperature changes. Multiplying by 2 can mean "twice as much change," and multiplying by $-1$ can mean "same size change, opposite direction." That is why signs matter in multiplication: they capture direction, not just size.

Division with rational numbers can be read as a fair-sharing or "how many groups" question, just like with whole numbers. The new part is that quantities can be fractional and signed, so we must keep track of both magnitude and direction carefully.

In this unit, the main goal is to make each operation feel meaningful first. Once the meaning is clear, standard rules become easier to remember and far less likely to be applied incorrectly.
