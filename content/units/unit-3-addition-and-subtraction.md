# Unit 3: Addition and Subtraction of Whole Numbers

## Concept Map
- Combine quantities -> Addition.
- Compare quantities or find what remains -> Subtraction.
- Regrouping relies on place-value exchanges.
- Inverse operations provide built-in answer checks.
- This unit supports later multiplication, division, and algebra balancing.

## Intuitive Explanation
Addition and subtraction are core tools for tracking change. We use them when we combine quantities, compare amounts, and decide what remains after something is removed. Whether we plan a budget, count inventory, or solve a story problem, these two operations appear every day.

Strong addition and subtraction skills also support later topics. Multi-digit multiplication builds on repeated addition ideas, and algebra depends on balancing by adding or subtracting the same amount on both sides.

### Place-Value View
Think about a school fundraiser.
- If Class A raised $248 and Class B raised $176, we **add** to find the total raised.
- If the total money is $500 and $289 was spent, we **subtract** to find what is left.

Place value is the key. In $248, the 2 means 200, the 4 means 40, and the 8 means 8. Addition and subtraction work best when we line up ones with ones, tens with tens, and hundreds with hundreds.

Regrouping (sometimes called carrying/borrowing) is an exchange of place values:
- 10 ones = 1 ten
- 10 tens = 1 hundred

So when a column is too large in addition, we regroup up. When a column is too small in subtraction, we regroup down.

## Formal Definition
For whole numbers $a$, $b$, and $c$:

- **Addition:** $a + b = c$ means the total of $a$ and $b$ is $c$.
- **Subtraction:** $a - b = c$ means removing $b$ from $a$ leaves $c$ (with $a \ge b$ in whole numbers).

Important properties:

1. **Commutative property of addition**
   $$
   a + b = b + a
   $$
2. **Associative property of addition**
   $$
   (a + b) + c = a + (b + c)
   $$
3. **Identity property of addition**
   $$
   a + 0 = a
   $$
4. **Inverse relationship**
   $$
   a - b = c \iff c + b = a
   $$

Standard algorithm reminders:
- Write numbers in vertical form with aligned place values.
- Add or subtract from right to left.
- Regroup carefully and record each regrouping step.

## Worked Examples
### Example 1: Multi-digit Addition with Regrouping
Compute $3{,}487 + 2{,}956$.

Set a quick estimate first: $3{,}500 + 3{,}000 \approx 6{,}500$.

1. Ones: $7 + 6 = 13$ → write 3, carry 1.
2. Tens: $8 + 5 + 1 = 14$ → write 4, carry 1.
3. Hundreds: $4 + 9 + 1 = 14$ → write 4, carry 1.
4. Thousands: $3 + 2 + 1 = 6$.

$$
3{,}487 + 2{,}956 = 6{,}443
$$
The exact answer is close to the estimate, so the result is reasonable.

### Example 2: Subtraction with Regrouping Across Zero
Compute $6{,}002 - 1{,}785$.

1. Ones: cannot do $2 - 5$, so regroup from tens/hundreds/thousands.
2. After regrouping across zeros, ones become 12, tens become 9, hundreds become 9, thousands become 5.
3. Ones: $12 - 5 = 7$.
4. Tens: $9 - 8 = 1$.
5. Hundreds: $9 - 7 = 2$.
6. Thousands: $5 - 1 = 4$.

$$
6{,}002 - 1{,}785 = 4{,}217
$$

Check:
$$
4{,}217 + 1{,}785 = 6{,}002
$$

### Example 3: Word Problem (Addition then Subtraction)
A library had 1,275 books. It bought 368 new books, then removed 149 damaged books. How many books are now in the library?

Step 1 (add new books):
$$
1{,}275 + 368 = 1{,}643
$$

Step 2 (subtract removed books):
$$
1{,}643 - 149 = 1{,}494
$$

So the library now has **1,494 books**.

### Example 4: Estimation to Check Reasonableness
Compute $5{,}909 - 2{,}487$ exactly, then estimate to check.

Exact:
$$
5{,}909 - 2{,}487 = 3{,}422
$$

Estimate by rounding to hundreds:
$$
5{,}900 - 2{,}500 = 3{,}400
$$

The exact answer $3{,}422$ is close to $3{,}400$, so it is reasonable.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them
- **Mistake 1: Misaligned place values** (for example, writing tens under hundreds).
  - **How to avoid it:** Use vertical format and check each column before computing.

- **Mistake 2: Forgetting a carried 1 in addition**.
  - **How to avoid it:** Circle each carry mark and cross it out only after using it.

- **Mistake 3: Incomplete regrouping across zeros in subtraction**.
  - **How to avoid it:** Rewrite the full row after regrouping so each changed digit is visible.

- **Mistake 4: Choosing subtraction for every “more” sentence in word problems**.
  - **How to avoid it:** First decide whether the problem is combine, compare, or remove, then pick the operation.

4. Compute mentally: $1{,}000 - 470$.
5. Estimate first: Is $3{,}192 + 4{,}806$ closer to $7{,}000$, $8{,}000$, or $9{,}000$?

## Summary and Key Takeaways
- Addition combines quantities; subtraction finds differences or what remains.
- Place-value alignment is essential for accurate multi-digit computation.
- Regrouping is an exchange of place values, not a trick.
- Estimation sets a target and helps catch mistakes quickly.
- Addition and subtraction are inverse operations and support later algebraic thinking.
